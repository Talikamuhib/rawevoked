The EEG pipeline, and why preprocessing exists

We run the whole thing once, end to end, on real data — and watch alpha waves appear the moment I close my eyes.

The one idea

EEG is a tiny brain signal buried under mountains of non-brain noise. Preprocessing isn't tidying up — it's the fight to pull microvolts of real neural activity out from under millivolts of junk sitting on top of it.

Every step below exists to remove one specific kind of contamination without damaging the real signal underneath. Once you hold that idea, each step stops being a mystery and becomes an obvious answer to the same question: what garbage am I removing, and why?

To make it concrete, we use the classic eyes-open vs. eyes-closed experiment. When you close your eyes, neurons in the occipital lobe — the visual area, at the back of your head — synchronise at around 10 Hz, producing alpha waves. Open your eyes and they collapse. Our goal is to make that alpha spike appear in a plot. The data comes from the public PhysioNet EEGBCI dataset, and MNE-Python downloads it for us — no files to hunt down.

Want to run it yourself while you read? The full annotated notebook is on GitHub — every code block below is in there, with the plots generated live. open the notebook ↗

01Load the data, and set the montage

what we do

Download two baseline runs for one subject — run 1 (eyes open) and run 2 (eyes closed) — then tell MNE where each electrode sat on the head.

why it matters

An EEG number is meaningless until you know where it was recorded. The montage maps channel names like O1 and Cz to physical scalp positions using the standard 10-20 system. Without it you can't make a head-map or interpret anything spatially. Think of the channel names as a coordinate system, not just labels.

raw_fnames = mne.datasets.eegbci.load_data(subjects=1, runs=[1, 2])
raw_open   = mne.io.read_raw_edf(raw_fnames[0], preload=True)
raw_closed = mne.io.read_raw_edf(raw_fnames[1], preload=True)

for raw in (raw_open, raw_closed):
    mne.datasets.eegbci.standardize(raw)   # clean channel names
    raw.set_montage("standard_1005")       # give every channel a 3D location

02Re-reference

what we do

Re-reference to the average of all channels.

why it matters

Every EEG voltage is a difference between the recording electrode and a reference point — there's no such thing as absolute brain voltage, only voltage relative to somewhere. If that somewhere is noisy, the noise leaks into every channel. The average reference uses the mean of all electrodes as a cleaner, more neutral baseline.

for raw in (raw_open, raw_closed):
    raw.set_eeg_reference("average", projection=False)

There are other choices here — linked mastoids, REST, single-electrode — and which one you pick depends mostly on how many electrodes you have. That's the whole of the next tutorial. For a 64-channel set like this, the average is a sensible default.

03Filter

what we do

Apply a band-pass filter keeping roughly 1–40 Hz.

why it matters

Three problems, handled at once: below ~1 Hz is slow drift from sweat and settling electrodes (removed by the high-pass edge); above ~40 Hz is fast muscle noise (removed by the low-pass edge); and our target, alpha at 8–12 Hz, sits comfortably inside the band we keep.

for raw in (raw_open, raw_closed):
    raw.filter(l_freq=1.0, h_freq=40.0)
the danger to respect

Filters have side effects. Too aggressive a high-pass can distort the very signal shapes you're trying to measure. The craft isn't "apply filter" — it's removing the noise band without eating the signal band next to it.

I'm skipping a dedicated notch filter here for simplicity; the 40 Hz low-pass already suppresses 50/60 Hz line noise. In a country with strong mains hum you'd add raw.notch_filter(50) — or 60 in the Americas.

04A note on ICA — and why I skip it this time

The full pipeline includes ICA to remove eye-blinks and heartbeat — it un-mixes the recording into independent sources so you can delete just the bad ones. It's the centrepiece of a later tutorial.

But here's an honest teaching moment: the alpha effect is so strong it survives minimal cleaning. For this first pass I skip ICA and still get a clear result. Re-running with ICA later and showing the plot get even cleaner makes a great follow-up — the contrast teaches more than either version alone. So: note that ICA belongs here in the order (after filtering, before epoching), and move on.

05The payoff — compute the power spectrum

what we do

Compute the power at each frequency for both conditions, focused on the occipital channels (O1, O2, Oz).

why occipital

Alpha is generated in the visual cortex at the back of the head. Looking at frontal channels would dilute the effect. Knowing where to look is half the analysis — and it's why we bothered with the montage in step 1.

occipital = ["O1", "O2", "Oz"]
psd_open   = raw_open.compute_psd(method="welch", fmin=1, fmax=40, picks=occipital)
psd_closed = raw_closed.compute_psd(method="welch", fmin=1, fmax=40, picks=occipital)

Now the moment of truth. If the physiology is real and the pipeline is sound, the eyes-closed curve should spike sharply around 10 Hz while the eyes-open curve stays flat there.

That is the entire point: a real, physiological brain signal, pulled cleanly out of the noise by a pipeline you now understand step by step.

06Where on the head is the alpha?

A spectrum proves alpha is there. A topographic map proves it's in the right place — the back of the head. We compute alpha-band power across all channels and paint it onto a head seen from above.

07What we learned

Walking the pipeline once, the shape of it:

  • Load + montage — give every electrode a position, or nothing spatial works.
  • Re-reference — voltage is always relative; choose a neutral baseline.
  • Filter — remove drift, muscle, and line noise without eating the signal.
  • (ICA) — un-mix and remove blinks and heartbeat; skippable when the effect is strong.
  • Analyse — go where the signal lives and let spectral estimation reveal what's invisible in the raw trace.
two ideas to carry forward

Order isn't arbitrary — each step assumes the last one ran. And every step is a trade-off, never free. Good preprocessing isn't running the most steps; it's knowing the minimum needed and what each one costs.
